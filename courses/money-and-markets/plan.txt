# Course Plan: Money & Markets — Economics and the forces that drive prosperity, inflation, and power

## Course Overview

**Title**: Money & Markets: Economics and the forces that drive prosperity, inflation, and power
**Course Slug**: money-and-markets
**Target Audience**: Listeners wanting to understand how economies work, from basic principles to complex global systems
**Core Goal**: Build economic literacy through expert interviews, progressing from foundational concepts to sophisticated understanding of markets, policy, and power dynamics

## Learning Progression

### Beginner Level: Economic Foundations & Core Concepts
**What**: Understanding money, markets, supply/demand, inflation basics, trade fundamentals
**Focus**:
- What is money and how does it work?
- Basic market mechanisms (supply/demand, prices, incentives)
- Inflation and deflation explained accessibly
- Why trade matters and comparative advantage
- How economic growth happens
- Role of banks and basic financial system

**Guest Types to Seek**:
- Accessible economists from top universities (explaining fundamentals clearly)
- Economic journalists/authors who write for general audiences
- Fed officials or central bankers explaining basic concepts
- Nobel laureates in "conversational" interview settings

**Listener Outcome**: Understand core economic vocabulary, mental models, and why economics matters to daily life

### Intermediate Level: Policy, Systems & Market Structures
**What**: Monetary policy, fiscal policy, market failures, behavioral economics, inequality
**Focus**:
- How central banks control economies (interest rates, quantitative easing)
- Government's role in the economy (taxation, spending, regulation)
- Market structures (monopoly, competition, externalities)
- Behavioral economics and decision-making
- Income/wealth inequality and its economic effects
- Financial crises and bubbles

**Guest Types to Seek**:
- Central bank researchers and policymakers
- Academic economists specializing in policy
- Economic historians explaining past crises
- Behavioral economists with research credentials
- Policy advisors with government experience

**Listener Outcome**: Understand how economic policy works, why markets fail, and how governments intervene

### Advanced Level: Global Economics, Power Dynamics & Modern Debates
**What**: International economics, institutional power, modern monetary theory debates, economic cycles, development
**Focus**:
- Global trade systems and currency dynamics
- Economic institutions and power (IMF, World Bank, WTO)
- Modern monetary theory vs. traditional economics
- Development economics and emerging markets
- Economic cycles and macro forecasting
- The political economy of inequality and power
- Future of capitalism debates

**Guest Types to Seek**:
- Leading macroeconomic researchers
- International economics specialists
- Economic philosophers/theorists
- Development economists with field experience
- Critics and reformers of economic orthodoxy (with credentials)

**Listener Outcome**: Grasp sophisticated debates, understand economic power structures, evaluate competing theories

### Professional Credentials
✅ "CFA" (Chartered Financial Analyst)
✅ "Managing Partner at [major firm]"
✅ "Portfolio Manager", "Chief Investment Officer"
✅ "Economist at [Goldman Sachs/Bloomberg/major institution]"

### Author/Thought Leader Credentials
✅ "Author of [bestselling economics book]"
✅ "New York Times/Wall Street Journal economics columnist"
✅ "Economic correspondent for [The Economist/FT/Bloomberg]"
✅ "TED Talk on economics with X million views"
✅ "Economics editor at [major publication]"

### Red Flags to Avoid
❌ No credentials mentioned - just "economist" or "expert"
❌ Self-proclaimed "investor" or "trader" without institutional backing
❌ "Financial guru", "wealth coach" (not academic economics)
❌ Cryptocurrency advocates without economic credentials
❌ Austrian economics podcasts (often ideological, not empirical)
❌ Libertarian think tanks without academic rigor
❌ "Common sense economics" without expertise
❌ Host monologue explaining concepts from Wikipedia or books
❌ Spiritual or mystical interpretations of economics
❌ Podcasts with <1000 followers unless exceptional guest

## Episode Evaluation Criteria

### Quality Scoring Framework

**⭐⭐⭐ EXCELLENT - Use These First:**
- Nobel laureate economist interviewed
- Top-10 university economics professor with clear research area
- Current/former Fed Chair, Governor, or Chief Economist
- Chief Economist at major institution (IMF, World Bank, Treasury)
- Published economist with bestselling book
- Top podcast (Planet Money, Freakonomics, EconTalk) interviewing credentialed expert
- Multiple strong credentials combined

**⭐⭐ GOOD - Solid Backup:**
- Economics professor at solid university
- Fed economist or regional Fed researcher
- Published economist at think tank (NBER, Brookings, Peterson)
- Economic journalist at top publication (Economist, FT, Bloomberg) with expertise
- CFA with academic or institutional background
- Practitioner with clear track record + credentials

**⭐ ACCEPTABLE - Use Only If Needed:**
- Legitimate economist but credentials unclear
- Policy role but not top-tier institution
- Business economist with some academic background
- Relevant expertise but not ideal positioning

**❌ REJECT - Never Use:**
- No identifiable credentials
- Host monologue (no expert guest)
- "Financial guru" or "wealth coach" types
- Ideological podcast without empirical rigor
- Cryptocurrency focus without economic credentials
- Self-help approach to economics
- Amateur podcast (<1000 followers)
- Spiritual/mystical content
- Wikipedia-based explanations

## Notes

- Economics has MANY highly credentialed experts - be selective
- Prioritize empirical economists over ideological perspectives
- Balance accessibility (beginner) with rigor (advanced)
- Economic policy is often debated - present multiple legitimate viewpoints
- Avoid cryptocurrency-focused content unless guest has traditional economics credentials
- Modern Monetary Theory is controversial - if included, present counterviews
- Inequality is important - seek economists who study it empirically
- International perspective matters - not just US-centric economics
